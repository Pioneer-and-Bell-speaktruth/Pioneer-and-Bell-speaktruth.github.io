Introduction (Part 1): Scripture Reading- Isaiah 53:3-7

• People often rush through the cross because they “already know the story.”

• But when we slow down and examine the biblical record like investigators, we see that:

• Every step is intentional.

• Every detail fulfills Scripture.

• Every moment reveals the depth of sin and the depth of God’s love.

• This is not just a tragic story; it is the turning point of human history.

• Today: we will walk through the events from sentencing to death — step by step — to see:

• What actually happened.

• How prophecy was fulfilled.

• What it means for how we live and obey today.

 

I. Jesus Is Sentenced (John 19:12–16; Luke 23:23–25; Matthew 27:24–25; Isaiah 53:8; Psalm 2)

A. Pilate’s final capitulation (He gave into demands)

• Pilate repeatedly declares Jesus innocent yet still hands Him over to be crucified.

• He “washes his hands” (Matt. 27:24) — a symbolic gesture that does not remove his guilt.

• The crowd cries, “His blood be on us and on our children!” (Matt. 27:25).

B. Prophetic significance

• Isaiah 53:8 — “By oppression and judgment he was taken away.”

• Psalm 2 — rulers conspire against the Lord and His Anointed.

• What looks like political chaos is actually God’s plan unfolding.

C. Lessons for today

• Human fear and political pressure still lead to moral compromise.

• Innocence does not guarantee justice in a corrupt system.

• Jesus is not a helpless victim; He willingly submits as the sacrifice for our sins.

• Application: Are we tempted to “wash our hands” instead of stand for what is right?

 

II. Jesus Is Scourged and Mocked (John 19:1–3; Mark 15:15–20; Isaiah 50:6; Psalm 22:7–8)

A. The scourging

• Roman scourging was designed to weaken and torture a person before crucifixion.

• Isaiah 50:6 — “I gave my back to those who strike…” — Jesus willingly endures this.

• This physical suffering underscores the cost of sin.

B. The mockery

• Jesus is dressed in a purple robe, given a crown of thorns, and a reed “scepter.”

• Soldiers bow in mock worship and strike Him.

• Psalm 22:7–8 predicts the mocking and scorn of the righteous sufferer.

C. Lessons for today

• Jesus understands humiliation and ridicule at a personal level.

• Mockery of righteousness is not new; faithful believers will face it too.

• God ultimately vindicates His servants, even when the world mocks them.

• Application: How do we respond when our faith is misunderstood or ridiculed?

 

III. The Journey to Golgotha (Luke 23:26–31; Luke 9:23)

A. Simon of Cyrene compelled

• Jesus, already weakened, cannot carry the crossbeam all the way.

• Simon is forced to carry it — a visible picture of “taking up the cross.”

• This moment combines Jesus’ real humanity with the call of discipleship.

B. Jesus’ warning to the daughters of Jerusalem

• Even in deep suffering, Jesus stops to speak to the women who are weeping.

• He warns of coming judgment on Jerusalem.

• He remains focused on God’s larger purposes, even while in pain.

C. Lessons for today

• Following Jesus involves carrying a cross — enduring hardship for His sake.

• True compassion is not suspended in times of personal suffering.

• Application: Do we still see others’ needs when we are hurting, like Jesus did?

 

IV. The Crucifixion (Luke 23:32–38; John 19:18–24; Isaiah 53:12; Psalm 22:18)

A. Nailed to the cross

• Jesus is crucified between two criminals — fulfilling Isaiah 53:12.

• Even in that moment, He prays: “Father, forgive them…” (Luke 23:34).

• Mercy flows out of Him in the middle of injustice.

B. The dividing of garments

• Soldiers cast lots for His clothing — exactly as Psalm 22:18 foretold.

• God’s word is fulfilled down to the smallest detail.

C. The inscription

• The charge above His head: “Jesus of Nazareth, King of the Jews.”

• Written in three languages — the truth about Jesus proclaimed to all who pass by.

• Even His enemies unintentionally preach His identity.

D. Lessons for today

• Jesus’ heart is to forgive even those who hurt Him.

• God is always in control, even when evil seems to be winning.

• Application: Are we willing to forgive others while we are still “on the cross” of our own pain?

 

V. The Sayings from the Cross (Luke 23:34, 43, 46; John 19:26–27, 28, 30; Matthew 27:46; Psalm 22:1; Psalm 69:21)

A. The seven sayings (brief overview)

·       Forgiveness — “Father, forgive them…” (Luke 23:34).

·       Assurance — “Today you will be with Me in Paradise.” (Luke 23:43).

·       Care — “Woman, behold your son… behold your mother.” (John 19:26–27).

·       Abandonment cry — “My God, My God, why have You forsaken Me?” (Matt. 27:46; Psalm 22:1).

·       Thirst — “I thirst.” (John 19:28; Psalm 69:21).

·       Completion — “It is finished.” (John 19:30).

·       Trust — “Father, into Your hands I commit My spirit.” (Luke 23:46).

B. Lessons for today

• Even in agony, Jesus is teaching, fulfilling Scripture, forgiving, and caring for others.

• His suffering is not random; it is purposeful, intentional, and redemptive.

• Application: What do our words sound like when we are under pressure or in pain?

 

VI. The Death of Jesus (Matthew 27:50–54; John 19:31–37; John 10:17–18)

A. The moment of death

• Jesus “gave up His spirit” (John 19:30) — His life is not taken; it is surrendered.

• John 10:17–18 — He has authority to lay it down and take it up again.

B. Miraculous signs

• The veil of the temple is torn in two — symbolizing open access to God.

• The earth quakes — creation responds to the death of its Maker.

• Graves open — a preview of resurrection power.

C. The centurion’s confession

• A hardened Roman soldier declares, “Truly this was the Son of God!” (Matt. 27:54).

• An outsider, not one of His disciples, recognizes His true identity.

D. Lessons for today

• Jesus’ death changes everything: access to God, our identity, and our hope.

• God often uses unexpected voices to speak truth.

• Application: What is our confession about Jesus in light of the cross?

 

Conclusion (Part 1):

• The trials show injustice.

• The scourging and mockery show the depth of human cruelty.

• The cross shows the depth of God’s love and the seriousness of sin.

• The death of Jesus opens the way for us to know God.

Takeaway:

• The cross is both a mirror and a window:

• A mirror showing us our sin and capacity for injustice.

• A window showing us God’s mercy if we are obedient to His will, and the sacrificial love of both the Father and the Son.

Becoming a Christian is simple:

·       We must hear the Word of God (Rom. 10:17)

·       Believe it. (Acts 8:37)

·       Repent of our sins, the very sends that sent our Savior to the cross. (2 Pet. 3:9)

·       Confess our faith before others and bring forth fruit (or evidence of) our repentance. (Acts 8:39)

·       Be immersed in the waters of baptism (1 Peter 3:21

Part 1 leaves us at the cross — humbled, grateful, and ready to see what God will do next in the burial and resurrection.
