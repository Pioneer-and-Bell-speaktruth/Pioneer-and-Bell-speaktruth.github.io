Introduction: Scripture Reading- Hebrews 10:1-4

From the very beginning, God has been a God who teaches through pictures. Before Israel ever held a Bible in their hands, they walked through a living illustration every single day. The tabernacle they saw, the priests they watched, the sacrifices they offered — all of it was God painting a portrait of something greater. These were not random rituals. They were shadows cast by a coming Savior. And this morning, we step into the Old Testament not to go backward, but to see how God was pointing forward to Christ all along.

 

1. The Tabernacle → God Dwelling With His People (Exodus 25–40)

A. The Physical Symbol (Old Law- Exodus 25:1-9)

- Exodus 25:8: “Let them make me a sanctuary (a holy place); that I may dwell among them.” (Ex. 13, 14; Numb. 14 God was with them with a pillar of cloud by day and a pillar of fire by night. This was a real physical manifestation and not just something the Israelites could see. Numb. 14 says that everyone would know that God was with them.)

- The tabernacle was:

  - Movable 

  - Central to Israel’s camp 

  - The place of sacrifice, mediation, and revelation 

- Every piece of furniture had symbolic meaning:

  - Lampstand → light 

  - Table of showbread → fellowship 

  - Altar of incense → prayer 

  - Ark → presence and mercy 

B. The Spiritual Reality (New Law- John 1:1-14)

- John 1:14 — “The Word was made flesh, and dwelt among us.” 

  - KJV “dwelt” = literally “tabernacled.” 

- Christ is the true meeting place between God and man. 

- The church becomes the dwelling place of God (Ephesians 2:19–22). 

- The individual Christian becomes a part of the temple (1 Corinthians 6:19–20).

C. Application

- God still desires to dwell with His people — not physically, but spiritually and representatively through His Word and through the church.

- Holiness belongs to the church as God’s temple — a calling we pursue together as His people.

- The church must reflect God’s presence, purity, and purpose as His dwelling place in the world.

2. The Priesthood → The Priesthood of All Obedient Believers

A. The Physical Symbol (Old Law- Exodus 29:1-9)

- Aaronic priesthood: 

  - Set apart by birth 

  - Consecrated with blood, oil, and garments 

  - Mediators between God and Israel 

B. The Spiritual Reality (New Law)

- Christ is our High Priest (Hebrews 4:14–16).

Heb 4:14-16 “(14) Seeing then that we have a great high priest, that is passed into the heavens, Jesus the Son of God, let us hold fast our profession. (15) For we have not an high priest which cannot be touched with the feeling of our infirmities; but was in all points tempted like as we are, yet without sin. (16) Let us therefore come boldly unto the throne of grace, that we may obtain mercy, and find grace to help in time of need.”

- Christians are a royal priesthood (1 Peter 2:5, 9). 

1Pe 2:5 “(5) Ye also, as lively stones, are built up a spiritual house, an holy priesthood, to offer up spiritual sacrifices, acceptable to God by Jesus Christ. (9) But ye are a chosen generation, a royal priesthood, an holy nation, a peculiar people; that ye should shew forth the praises of him who hath called you out of darkness into his marvellous light:”

 

 

- Priestly functions now fulfilled spiritually:

  - Offering spiritual sacrifices (Romans 12:1; Hebrews 13:15–16) 

Rom 12:1 (1) I beseech you therefore, brethren, by the mercies of God, that ye present your bodies a living sacrifice, holy, acceptable unto God, which is your reasonable service.

Heb 13:15-16 (15) By him therefore let us offer the sacrifice of praise to God continually, that is, the fruit of our lips giving thanks to his name. (16) But to do good and to communicate forget not: for with such sacrifices God is well pleased.

  - Interceding in prayer 

  - Teaching God’s Word 

  - Living consecrated lives

C. Application

- Every Christian has a ministry. 

- Worship is not spectator‑based; it is participatory. 

- Holiness is not optional—it is priestly duty.

3. Animal Sacrifices → The Perfect Sacrifice of Christ

A. The Physical Symbol (Old Law- Leviticus 1:1-17)

- Daily, weekly, monthly, yearly sacrifices (Leviticus 1–7). 

- Blood was central: 

Leviticus 1:11 “And he shall kill it on the side of the altar northward before the LORD: and the priests, Aaron's sons, shall sprinkle his blood round about upon the altar.”

- Sacrifices were:

  - Repetitive 

  - Imperfect 

  - Temporary 

  - Substitutionary

B. The Spiritual Reality (New Law- Hebrews 10:1-14)

- Hebrews 10:1 — “The law having a shadow of good things to come.” 

- Christ’s sacrifice:

  - Once; never to be done again (Hebrews 10:10–14) 

         - ephapax (G2178) related to Jude 1:3 hapax

         - There remaineth no more sacrifice for sin (Heb. 10:26)

-  Based on this context, the Hebrew writer is referring to how many times the sacrifice would need to be made. The priests under the old law were every year, Jesus only had to do it once.

- Jn 3:16 reminds us that salvation was offered to the whole world, but that is not in this context.

- Perfect (Complete, Heb. 5:8-9)

  - Final 

  - Cleansing the conscience, not just the flesh (I Pet. 3:21; I Jn 1:7)

C. Application

- Sin is serious—it requires blood. 

- Grace is costly—never cheap. 

- Our response must be gratitude, obedience, and holiness.

Conclusion:

·      When we look at the tabernacle, the priesthood, and the sacrifices, we’re reminded that God has always been pointing His people toward something greater. These Old Testament symbols were never the final answer — they were preparing the way for Christ.

o  The tabernacle showed that God wanted to dwell with His people.

o  The priesthood showed that we needed a mediator.

o  The sacrifices showed the seriousness of sin and the need for atonement.

o  In Jesus, all three come together in their fullest meaning. He is God with us. He is our High Priest. He is the perfect sacrifice.

·      So our conclusion this morning is simple:

o  Everything the Old Testament pointed toward, we now find in Christ.

o  And tonight, we’ll continue to see how the remaining symbols lead us to the same place — to the One who fulfills every shadow with His substance.
