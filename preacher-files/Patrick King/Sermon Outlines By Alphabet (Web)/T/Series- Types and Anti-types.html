The Passover Lamb – A Shadow of Christ’s Sacrifice [Not Using]

 

Text: Exodus 12:1-14; John 1:29; 1 Corinthians 5:7

Introduction:

- The Passover lamb in the Old Testament was a shadow of the ultimate sacrifice of Jesus Christ, the Lamb of God.

- The blood of the lamb in Exodus saved the Israelites from judgment, just as the blood of Christ saves us from sin and eternal separation from God.

I. The Significance of the Passover Lamb

- Exodus 12:3-6: The lamb had to be without blemish (a type of Christ’s sinlessness). 

- The blood was applied to the doorposts for protection (Exodus 12:7). 

- The lamb’s death spared the firstborn (Exodus 12:13).

II. Christ, Our Passover Lamb

- John 1:29: Jesus is introduced as "the Lamb of God, which taketh away the sin of the world." 

- 1 Corinthians 5:7: "For even Christ our passover is sacrificed for us."

III. The Fulfillment in Christ’s Sacrifice

- The Old Testament Passover foreshadowed Christ's ultimate sacrifice on the cross. 

- Hebrews 9:22: Without shedding of blood is no remission. Christ’s blood is the final atonement.

Conclusion: 

- Just as the Passover lamb provided physical deliverance for Israel, Jesus’ sacrifice provides spiritual deliverance for all who believe. 

- Apply the blood of Christ to your life and experience His salvation.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

The Bronze Serpent – A Shadow of Christ’s Redemptive Power [12/1 AM] Done

 

Text: Numbers 21:4-9; John 3:14-15

Introduction: 

- The bronze serpent lifted up in the wilderness by Moses was a powerful symbol of God’s provision for physical healing and points to the ultimate healing through Christ's death on the cross.

I. The Israelites' Sin and Judgment

- Numbers 21:6: The people were bitten by fiery serpents as a judgment for their sin and disobedience. 

- Sin brings death and separation from God, symbolized by the serpent.

II. God's Remedy – The Bronze Serpent

- Numbers 21:8: God instructed Moses to make a bronze serpent and lift it up, so that anyone who looked at it would live. 

- The bronze serpent represents Christ, who would be lifted up on the cross (John 3:14-15).

III. Jesus Lifted Up on the Cross

- John 3:14-15: Just as the serpent was lifted up, so must the Son of Man be lifted up, that whosoever believes in Him should not perish but have eternal life. 

- Christ’s crucifixion brings healing from the serpent of sin and eternal death.

Conclusion: 

- Just as the Israelites were saved by faith in looking to the bronze serpent, we are saved by faith in looking to Jesus Christ, crucified for our sins.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

The Manna from Heaven – A Shadow of the Bread of Life [Not Using]

 

Text: Exodus 16:4-15; John 6:31-35

Introduction: 

- The manna provided by God in the wilderness was a physical sustenance for the Israelites, yet it pointed forward to the true Bread of Life—Jesus Christ, who satisfies the deepest hunger of our souls.

I. The Provision of Manna

- Exodus 16:4-15: God sent manna from heaven each day to sustain the Israelites in the wilderness. 

- Manna was a miraculous provision that met their physical needs.

II. Jesus – The True Bread of Heaven

- John 6:31-33: The manna was a type, but Jesus is the true bread from heaven. 

- John 6:35: Jesus declared, "I am the bread of life," offering spiritual sustenance that satisfies forever.

III. The Manna and Christ’s Sacrifice

- John 6:51: "I am the living bread which came down from heaven: if any man eat of this bread, he shall live forever." 

- Christ’s body, broken for us, is the true manna that gives eternal life.

Conclusion: 

- Just as the Israelites had to gather the manna daily, we must daily partake of Christ, our Bread of Life, through faith and communion with Him.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

The Tabernacle – A Shadow of God Dwelling Among Us [12/1 PM] Done

 

Text: Exodus 25:8; John 1:14; Hebrews 9:11-12

Introduction: 

- The Tabernacle in the Old Testament was a mobile dwelling place for God among His people, pointing to the ultimate fulfillment of God’s presence in the person of Jesus Christ.

I. The Purpose of the Tabernacle

- Exodus 25:8: God commanded the building of the Tabernacle as a place for His presence to dwell among the Israelites. 

- It symbolized God’s desire to be near His people.

II. Jesus – God Dwelling Among Us

- John 1:14: "And the Word was made flesh, and dwelt among us" – Jesus is the true tabernacle, the presence of God in human form. 

- Hebrews 9:11-12: Jesus entered the heavenly tabernacle, offering Himself as the perfect sacrifice for our sins.

III. The Tabernacle and Christ’s Fulfillment

- The Tabernacle’s layout, its sacrifices, and its furnishings all point to Christ and His work of redemption. 

- Through Jesus, God dwells among us permanently and offers access to His presence.

Conclusion: 

- As the Tabernacle was the meeting place between God and man, so Jesus Christ is the ultimate meeting place, where we can experience God’s presence and salvation.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Melchizedek – A Shadow of Christ’s Priesthood [12/29 AM]

 

Text: Genesis 14:18-20; Hebrews 7:1-3

Introduction: 

- Melchizedek, the king of Salem and priest of the Most High God, serves as a mysterious figure whose priesthood points to the eternal and perfect priesthood of Jesus Christ.

I. Melchizedek’s Unique Priesthood

- Genesis 14:18-20: Melchizedek blesses Abraham and is both a king and a priest, a rare and unique combination. 

- His priesthood is not based on genealogy or law, but on the power of an endless life.

II. Jesus – The Great High Priest 

- Hebrews 7:1-3: Jesus is a priest forever after the order of Melchizedek, a priesthood that is eternal and unchanging. 

- Jesus’ priesthood is superior to the Levitical priesthood because it does not end and it brings a better covenant.

III. The Eternal Nature of Christ’s Priesthood

- Hebrews 7:24-25: Jesus lives forever to make intercession for us, ensuring that His priesthood is effective for all eternity. 

- Melchizedek’s priesthood pointed forward to the eternal and perfect work of Christ on our behalf.

Conclusion: 

- As Melchizedek's priesthood foreshadowed Christ's, we have an eternal High Priest who offers an unchanging, perfect sacrifice for our sins.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

The Sacrificial System – A Shadow of Jesus, Our Perfect Sacrifice [Not Using]

 

Text: Leviticus 16:29-34; Hebrews 10:1-10

Introduction: 

- The sacrificial system, especially the Day of Atonement, was a shadow of the ultimate sacrifice Jesus would make for sin, once for all.

I. The Sacrificial System in the Old Testament

- Leviticus 16:29-34: The Day of Atonement involved sacrifices to atone for the sins of the people. 

- The blood of animals covered sin temporarily, pointing to the need for a perfect sacrifice.

II. Jesus – The Ultimate Sacrifice

- Hebrews 10:1-10: The law could never make perfect those who came to it, but Christ came to do God’s will, offering Himself as the perfect sacrifice. 

- Hebrews 9:12-14: Christ entered the holy place with His own blood, securing eternal redemption for us.

III. The Finality of Christ’s Sacrifice

- Jesus' sacrifice is once for all, and it completely removes the penalty of sin (Hebrews 10:10). 

- Christ is the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world (John 1:29).

Conclusion: 

- The Old Testament sacrifices were a shadow pointing to the ultimate sacrifice of Christ. Through His death, we are fully reconciled to God.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Jonah in the Whale – A Shadow of Christ’s Death and Resurrection [12/1 AM] Done

 

Text: Jonah 1:17; Matthew 12:40

Introduction: 

- Jonah’s experience in the belly of the great fish (whale) was a sign pointing to Christ’s death, burial, and resurrection.

I. Jonah’s Time in the Belly of the Fish

- Jonah 1:17: Jonah was swallowed by a great fish and remained in its belly for three days and three nights. 

- This was a time of judgment and deliverance for Jonah.

II. Jesus’ Death, Burial, and Resurrection

- Matthew 12:40: Jesus compares His death and resurrection to Jonah’s time in the belly of the fish. 

- Just as Jonah was three days and nights in the fish, so Jesus would be in the heart of the earth before rising again.

III. Christ’s Resurrection: The Ultimate Sign

- Jonah’s deliverance from the fish is a type of Christ’s resurrection, which offers hope of new life for all believers.

Conclusion: 

- Jonah’s story foreshadowed Christ’s death and resurrection, which gives us eternal life and victory over death.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

The Law – A Shadow of Grace and Truth in Christ [12/1 PM] Done

 

Text: Exodus 20:1-17; John 1:17

Introduction: 

- The Law given to Moses was holy and righteous, but it could not save. It pointed to Christ, who fulfills the Law and offers grace and truth.

I. The Purpose of the Law

- Exodus 20:1-17: The Ten Commandments show God’s standard of holiness. 

- Galatians 3:24: The Law was a schoolmaster to bring us to Christ, showing our need for a Savior.

II. Christ – The Fulfillment of the Law

- Matthew 5:17: Jesus came not to abolish the Law but to fulfill it. 

- John 1:17: The Law came by Moses, but grace and truth came by Jesus Christ.

III. Grace and Truth in Christ 

- Romans 10:4: Christ is the end of the law for righteousness to everyone that believeth. 

- In Christ, we receive grace, which empowers us to live according to God’s will.

Conclusion: 

- The Law points us to Christ, who fulfills its demands and offers us salvation through grace and truth.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

The Covenant with Abraham – A Shadow of Salvation by Faith [12/29 AM]

 

Text: Genesis 12:1-3; Romans 4:1-3

Introduction: 

- God’s covenant with Abraham promised blessings to all nations through him, pointing to salvation by faith in Jesus Christ.

I. God’s Covenant with Abraham 

- Genesis 12:1-3: God promises to bless Abraham and make his descendants a great nation. 

- The covenant was based on faith and trust in God’s promises.

II. Faith, the Key to the Covenant 

- Romans 4:1-3: Abraham’s righteousness came by faith, not by works. 

- Abraham believed God’s promise, and it was counted to him for righteousness.

III. Christ’s Role in the Promise 

- Galatians 3:16-18: The promises were made to Abraham and his seed, and Jesus is the fulfillment of that promise. 

- Salvation comes through faith in Christ, just as Abraham was justified by faith.

Conclusion: 

- Abraham’s covenant points to salvation by faith in Christ, who fulfills God’s promises to all who believe.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

The Day of Atonement – A Shadow of Christ’s Final Atonement [12/29 PM]

 

Text: Leviticus 16:1-34; Hebrews 9:11-14

Introduction: 

- The Day of Atonement, when the high priest made sacrifices for the sins of the people, foreshadowed the perfect atonement Jesus would make once for all.

I. The Day of Atonement and the High Priest

- Leviticus 16:1-34: The high priest would enter the Holy of Holies, offering sacrifices to atone for the sins of the people. 

- This was a necessary ritual for the forgiveness of sin.

II. Christ, Our Perfect High Priest 

- Hebrews 9:11-14: Christ entered the heavenly sanctuary and offered His own blood, securing eternal redemption for us. 

- Jesus’ sacrifice is the final and perfect atonement for sin.

III. The Fullness of Christ’s Atonement 

- Hebrews 10:10-14: Christ’s sacrifice was once for all, fully satisfying the wrath of God and securing eternal life for believers.

Conclusion: 

- The Day of Atonement was a shadow of the ultimate atonement made by Jesus Christ, who, through His blood, brings full forgiveness and reconciliation to God.
